\section{Introduction}

In physical oceanography, the majority of available data is acquired by satellites that cannot measure the interior ocean state. The recent launch of the Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT) mission~\parencite{fu2024} has paved the way for sea surface reconstruction with unprecedented resolution. However, due to the shape of its orbit, this satellite covers the entire surface of the globe in 21 days. Although most parts of the globe are visited multiple times within this 21d-period, many gaps remain in the collected data. The reconstruction of sea-surface dynamics through partial observation has been investigated for a long time. In the late 1980s, some physics-motivated flow reconstruction methods were developed, either focusing on surface~\parencite{mey1987,holland1989,cong1995,cooper1996,cong1998} or subsurface~\parencite{hurlburt1986,haines1991} flow.
In the 1990s, statistical methods such as optimal interpolation~\parencite{rienecker1991,traon1998} emerged, forming the basis of the DUACS product which remains a widely used real-time gridded ocean product. This interpolation method uses only observation data to reconstruct surface ocean state.

To make use of theoretical models of ocean surface dynamics, data assimilation (DA) techniques started to be applied to the ocean system. In order to reduce reconstruction errors, DA combines both simulated and observed data while accounting for their specific sources of error~\parencite{lahoz2010}. A wide variety of methods have been developed and applied to the reconstruction of surface ocean dynamics~\parencite{martin2025}. In DA methods, so-called reduced-order models are used to propagate in time the system state. These models use simplified frameworks to emulate ocean system dynamics, allowing to perform efficiently complex DA algorithms. Around the midlatitudes, a simplified framework of choice is the quasi-geostrophic (QG) theory. This framework, developed in the 1950s~\parencite{charney1950,charney1953}, describes the behavior of flows around the geostrophic balance through the material conservation of potential vorticity (PV) by the geostrophic flow. Such theory has paved the way for many simplified numerical models, whether atmospheric or oceanic~\parencite{phillips1951,held1995}. Although it describes 3D dynamics, it can be discretized by stacking layers of fluid with constant vertical properties, yielding the multilayer QG framework~\parencite{vallis2006}. The ability of such framework in reconstructing sublayer flow has been widely investigated in the late 1980s~\parencite{hurlburt1986,haines1991}, but satisfying reconstructions are achieved after numerous surface observations have assimilated into the models. Regarding surface flow reconstruction-only, assimilation into a two-layer QG model has been experimented by~\cite{cong1998} and showed encouraging results. However, the lack of subsurface direct observations (and thus of precise estimation for initial sublayer flow state) has motivated the use of reduced-gravity (RG) QG model in data assimilation. This framework is directly derived from the multilayer one by considering deep ocean flow to be at rest. This additional simplification offers an evolution equation for the surface flow without requiring any information on subsurface state. These specific characteristics naturally led to the choice of the RG framework for modeling surface dynamics.

Using a RG model,~\cite{ubelmann2015} presented the so-called dynamical interpolation method that consist in refining the optimal interpolation reconstruction by using a RG model to benefit from its ability to render non-linear dynamics. This method has been able to improve the reconstruction of the flow~\parencite{ubelmann2016}. In order to account for any source of error in the RG model, the VarDyn framework~\parencite{leguillou2023,leguillou2025} introduces an external forcing into the RG advection equation. Such forcing is then estimated through variational assimilation to approach the rich and complex dynamics of the ocean surface. However, in both methods remains the approximation that the flow is at rest below the ocean surface.

Motivated by a rewriting of the top layer QG equations, we tackle truncation error through a parameterization of the sublayer stream function. We introduce an external forcing in the RG equations which represents the advection of sublayer stream function by surface flow. In a context of partial surface observations, variational assimilation is then performed to find the sublayer field that allows the most accurate reconstruction of the surface flow. As our method provides a direct estimation for sublayer stream function, we are also able to replace the usual background term of the loss function by a physics-based regularization enforcing multilayer QG dynamics of the surface flow. Method validation is then performed through two experiments: comparison to a three-layer QG model to monitor truncation-only error mitigation, and comparison to a primitive equation simulation to assess the model's reconstruction capabilities of a richer dynamics flow. 


\section{The multilayer QG theory}

QG dynamics relies on the material conservation of potential vorticity (PV) by geostrophic flow. As PV is related to the stream function through an elliptic operator, the full geostrophic flow can be recovered from the PV field through the inversion of such operator~\parencite{hoskins1985}. Multilayer QG, the vertical discretization of the continuously stratified QG equation, is obtained by stacking layers of fluid with constant vertical properties~\parencite{pedlosky1987,vallis2006}. In the following we will recall these equations in a $N$-layer fluid. Using $\vect{\cdot}$ to represent layered fields ($\vect{\chi}= \transpose{\begin{bmatrix}\chi_1, \dots, \chi_N\end{bmatrix}}$), we denote

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vect{\rho}$ the reference layer densities,
    \item $\vect{H}$ the reference layer thickness,
    \item $\vect{g}$ the reduced gravities ($g_i = g\left(\rho_{i}-\rho_{i-1}\right)/\rho_1$, with the convention $g_1 = g$),
    \item $\vect{\sf}$ the stream function vector
    \item $\vect{\pv}$ the PV vector
\end{itemize}
\bigskip
Introducing the horizontal gradient $\grad = \transpose{\begin{bmatrix} \pdvx & \pdvy \end{bmatrix}}$ along with the perpendicular horizontal gradient $\gradperp = \transpose{\begin{bmatrix} -\pdvy & \pdvx \end{bmatrix}}$, the free-surface multilayer QG equations (in the absence of external forcing) in a layer $i$ read

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        \pv_i = \lap \sf_i - f_0^2 {\left(\mat{A} \vect{\sf}\right)}_i, \label{def:pv-ml}\\
        \pdvt{\pv_i} + \gradperp \sf_i \cdot \grad \pv_i = 0\label{def:pv-adv},
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}

where the stretching matrix $\mat{A}$, is given by

\begin{equation}
    \mat{A} = \begin{bmatrix}
            \frac{1}{H_1 g_1} + \frac{1}{H_1 g_2} & -\frac{1}{H_1 g_2} & \cdot & \cdot \\
            -\frac{1}{H_2 g_2} & \frac{1}{H_2 g_2} + \frac{1}{H_2 g_3} & -\frac{1}{H_2 g_3} & \cdot \\
            \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 
            \cdot & \cdot & -\frac{1}{H_N g_N} & \frac{1}{H_N g_N} \\ 
        \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}

Direct observations through satellite sensing are only able to measure sea surface height (SSH), which can be matched to top layer stream function $\sf_1$. Remote sensing alone can not provide direct measurement on the sublayer flow $\sf_2$. However, the top layer PV ($\pv_1$), the dynamics of which govern surface flow, depends on the stream function from layers $1$ and $2$ as

\begin{equation}\label{def:pv-top-ml}
    \pv_1 = \lap{\sf_1} - \frac{f_0^2}{H_1 g_1} \sf_1 - \frac{f_0^2}{H_1 g_2} \left(\sf_1 - \sf_2\right).
\end{equation}

To overcome such limitation, the RG framework considers the sublayer to be at rest, hence $\sf_2 = 0$. The corresponding top-layer PV $\pv^{RG}$ expression yields

\begin{equation}\label{def:pv-rg}
    \pv^{RG} = \lap{\sf_1} - f_0^2 \left(\frac{1}{H_1 g_1} + \frac{1}{H_1 g_2}\right) \sf_1,
\end{equation}

which is advected by $\sf_1$, hence,

\begin{equation}
    \pdvt{\pv^{RG}} +  \gradperp{\sf_1}\cdot \grad{\pv^{RG}} = 0.
\end{equation}

This is the formulation of the RG model which was used in the first time to perform dynamical interpolation~\parencite{ubelmann2015,ubelmann2016}. Benefiting from the ability of the QG model to generate non-linear dynamics, such method has been able to outperform statistics-based interpolation. However, due to the truncated QG equations, significant errors can emerge when performing optimal interpolation using solely the RG model, as pointed out in~\cite{manucharyan2021}. In order to address this limitation, the VarDyn framework~\parencite{leguillou2023,leguillou2025} acknowledges the model error by introducing an external forcing, $\epsilon$, into the advection equation,

\begin{equation}
    \pdvt{\pv^{RG}} + J\left(\sf_1, \pv^{RG}\right) = \epsilon.
\end{equation}

However, given the QG PV definition, the top layer PV can be written

\begin{equation}
    \pv_1 = \pv^{RG} + \frac{f_0^2}{H_1 g_2} \sf_2.
\end{equation}

Using this decomposition into the top layer PV advection equation yields

\begin{subequations}\label{def:qg-rg-adv-corr}
    \begin{align}
        \pdvt{\pv^{RG}} + J\left(\sf_1, \pv^{RG}\right) & = - \frac{f_0^2}{H_1 g_2} \left[\pdvt{\sf_2} + \gradperp \sf_1 \cdot \grad \sf_2 \right] \\
        & = - \frac{f_0^2}{H_1 g_2} \Dt{\sf_2}.
    \end{align}
    
\end{subequations}

From this expression, we note that the usual RG model advection matches the multilayer formulation when $\Dt*{\sf_2}=0$. Addressing the truncation error of the RG model resumes to emulating the right-hand side of~\cref{def:qg-rg-adv-corr}. Since, $\sf_1$ is the prognostic variable of the model, computing $\Dt*{\sf_2}$ boils down to estimating $\sf_2$. Denoting $\tilde{\sf}_2$ such estimation, we define the corrected RG advection by

\begin{equation}
    \pdvt{\pv^{RG}} + J\left(\sf_1, \pv^{RG}\right) = - \frac{f_0^2}{H_1 g_2} \Dt{\tilde{\sf}_2}.
\end{equation}

In the following, this model will be referred to as SurfML (Surface Multi-Layer). Additionaly to explicitly addressing the RG-related truncation error, this method also provides a proxy for the sublayer stream function, through $\tilde{\sf}_2$. Our specific parametrization of $\tilde{\sf}_2$ will be detailed in~\cref{sec:forward-model}. In the forthcoming, all numerical experiments were conducted using a Pytorch implementation of the multilayer QG equations derived from~\cite{thiry2024} (extended to withstand non-zero boundary conditions).

\section{Variational assimilation}

We consider a non-linear, time-evolving system whose state at time $t$ is given by $x_t^{\mathrm{true}}$. The observation operator $\mathcal{H}$ allows  gathering a (discrete) observation $\symbf{y}_t = \mathcal{H}\left(x_t^{\mathrm{true}}\right)$ on the system state at time $t$. We introduce the propagator $\mathcal{M}$, which provides a (discrete) proxy $\symbf{x}_t = \mathcal{M}\left(\theta \ | \ t\right)$ for $x_t^{\mathrm{true}}$, given the control vector $\theta$ which may include any parameter the propagator dynamics depends on (initial and boundary condition, model parameters\dots). Observations drawn from the true state can be compared to their counterpart generated using $\mathcal{H}\circ\mathcal{M}$. Given a set of observations ${\left(\symbf{y}_i\right)}_{i \in \{1, \dots, N_{\mathrm{obs}}\}}$ evaluated at times ${\left(t_i\right)}_{i \in \{1, \dots, N_{\mathrm{obs}}\}}$, the performance of $\mathcal{M}$ in reproducing the evolution of the system can be evaluated by the observation loss

\begin{equation}
    \loss{obs}\left(\theta\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{obs}}}{\lVert \symbf{y}_{i} - \mathcal{H} \circ \mathcal{M}\left(\theta \ | \ t_i\right)\rVert}^2_{\mat{R}^{-1}},
\end{equation}

with the observation error covariance matrix $\mat{R}$. Variational assimilation reduces to finding

\begin{equation}
    \theta^\star = \arg \min_{\theta}\loss{obs}\left(\theta\right).
\end{equation}

However, there are no guarantees that enough data is available for the minimization problem to be well constrained. Therefore, provided a prior estimate of the control vector, $\theta^b$, and its error covariance matrix, $\mat{B}$, the cost function is usually regularized by penalizing the deviation of $\theta$ from $\theta^b$. Introducing the regularization loss

\begin{equation}
    \loss{reg}\left(\theta\right) = {\lVert \theta - \theta^b \rVert}^2_{\mat{B}^{-1}},
\end{equation}

the cost function rewrites

\begin{equation}
    \loss\left(\theta\right) = \loss{obs}\left(\theta\right) + \loss{reg}\left(\theta\right).
\end{equation}

Supposing we can estimate the gradient $\grad \loss$ of the loss function, minimization can be performed through gradient descent.  Regarding our specific experimentations, since all models and operation are implemented using Pytorch, we benefit from the library's built-in automatic differentiation~\parencite{paszke2017} to both retrieve loss function gradient and perform a descent algorithm.



\section{The forward model}\label{sec:forward-model}
Hereafter we detail the specificities of the SurfML model and of the sublayer parameterization which was used as propagator in the variational assimilation algorithm.

\subsection{Sublayer parameterization}

In order to keep a reasonable number of parameters for the parameterization of $\tilde{\sf}_2$, we use a reduced-order space to represent its value. We have decided to express $\tilde{\sf}_2$ over a quite simple space so that SurfML performances could not be attributed to the structure of the parameterization rather than the actual error model. Therefore, the spatio-temporal behavior of $\tilde{\sf}_2$ is prescribed through a partition of unity of Gaussian envelopes, both in time and space. Denoting $N_{T}$ the number of time-related envelopes and $N_{XY}$ the number of space-related envelopes at a given time $i$, for given $i \in \{1,\dots,N_T\}$ and $j \in \{1,\dots,N_{XY}\}$, we define

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        E^T_{i}\left(t\right) & = \exp\left[-{\left(\frac{t-t_i}{\sigma_T}\right)}^2\right], \\
        E^{XY}_{j}\left(x,y\right) & = \exp\left[-{\left(\frac{x-x_j}{\sigma_X}\right)}^2\right]\exp\left[-{\left(\frac{y-y_j}{\sigma_Y}\right)}^2\right],
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}

where $t_i, x_j, y_j$ are the locations of the envelope centers and $\sigma_T, \sigma_X, \sigma_Y$ their standard deviation. We also define $E^T = \sum_{i=1}^{N_T} E^T_i$ and $E^{XY} = \sum_{i=1}^{N_{XY}} E^{XY}_j$. Using these notations, the overall decomposition of $\tilde{\sf}_2$ at time $t$ and location $\left(x,y\right)$ reads

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\sf}_2\left(t,x,y\right) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N_T}\sum_{j=1}^{N_{XY}}  c_{i,j} E^T_{i}\left(t\right)E^{XY}_{j}\left(x,y\right)}{E^T\left(t\right) E^{XY}\left(x,y\right)},
\end{equation}

where the ${\left(c_{i,j}\right)}_{i \in \{1, \dots, N_T\}, j \in \{1, \dots, N_{XY}\}}$ are coefficients inferred through cost function minimization. Setting all coefficient to 0 restores the dynamics of a flow following the RG assumption and is chosen as the initial condition for the parameterization. Envelopes are regularly spaced over both time and space and their standard deviations are chosen so that consecutive envelopes cross at $\qty{-3}{\decibel}$. Hence, considering consecutive time-related (resp., space-related) envelopes to be distant from $\Delta t$ (resp., $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$), their standard deviations are given by

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        \sigma_T & = \frac{\Delta t}{\sqrt{2\ln{2}}}, \\
        \sigma_X & = \frac{\Delta x}{\sqrt{2\ln{2}}}, \\
        \sigma_Y & = \frac{\Delta y}{\sqrt{2\ln{2}}}.
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}

Such parameterization allows representing a smooth field while enabling the emergence of some rather small scale patterns.

\subsection{Physics-based regularization}

Following~\cref{def:pv-adv}, PV advection in the layer 2 reads

\begin{equation}
    \pdvt*{\pv_2} + \gradperp \sf_2 \cdot \grad \pv_2 = 0.
\end{equation}

However, as the shape of our model error yields a proxy for $\sf_2$, we define the sublayer PV using the truncation of its multilayer definition (we consider $\sf_3 = 0$), retrieving the following proxy for $\pv_2$,

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\pv}_2 = \lap{\tilde{\sf}_2} - \frac{f_0^2}{H_2 g_2}\left(\tilde{\sf}_2 - \sf_1\right).
\end{equation}

We can compute an estimation of the sublayer advection residual with $\pdvt*{\tilde{\pv}_2} + \gradperp \tilde{\sf}_2 \cdot \grad \tilde{\pv}_2$. In order to enforce physical constraints during the optimization process, we introduce a physics-based regularization, 

\begin{equation}
    \loss{reg}\left(\sf_1, \tilde{\sf}_2\right) = {\lVert \pdvt*{\tilde{\pv}_2} + \gradperp \tilde{\sf}_2 \cdot \grad \tilde{\pv}_2 \rVert}^2,
\end{equation}

and we define the overall cost function with

\begin{equation}\label{def:surfml-reg}
    \loss \left(\tilde{\sf}_2\right) = \loss{obs}\left(\tilde{\sf}_2\right) + \gamma \loss{reg}\left(\tilde{\sf}_2\right),
\end{equation}

where $\gamma$ is an hyperparameter driving the relative importance of the regularization over the observation loss. Replacing the usual background state related regularization by this physics-based formulation is inspired by physics-informed neural networks (PINN) methodology~\parencite{raissi2019} and PINN-derived data assimilation approaches have proven the relevancy of regularizing using physical constraint~\parencite{ugur2025,oh2025}.

Additionally, switching to a physics-based regularization removes the need for an a priori guess of the parameter. Since our method is based on reconstructing a proxy for the sublayer stream function, retrieving a non-trivial estimation for $\sf_2$ remains challenging. A possible estimation would have been to penalize $\tilde{\sf}_2$ from deviating from 0, thus preventing the model from stirring away from the RG framework. It would have also been possible to enforce a barotropic flow by penalizing deviation from $\sf_1$. However, both of these options pull the model toward one-layer dynamics whereas the physics-derived regularization allows for two-layer-like dynamics while still allowing the system to fall back to $\tilde{\sf}_2 \simeq 0$ or $\tilde{\sf}_2 \simeq \sf_1$ when appropriate. 

It is also worth emphasizing that introducing this regularization does not strictly enforce the conservation of PV in the layer. It guides the model towards finding an expression for $\tilde{\sf}_2$ that approaches the conservation $\tilde{\pv}_2$. However, as there are no guarantees that $\tilde{\pv}_2$ should be strictly conserved ($\tilde{\sf}_2$ is not exact and $\tilde{\pv}_2$ is computed through the truncation of the multilayer equations), this formulation allows for some flexibility on said conservation. 

Furthermore, if we were to assimilate an initial condition $\sf_2^0$ for the bottom layer stream function in two-layer QG model, constraining the minimization problem would yield the question of finding a background estimation for $\sf_2^0$. Once again, two natural choices would be the following: considering $0$ or $\sf_1^0$ (or a linear combination of both, as done by~\cite{cong1995}), hence leaning toward either a surface intensified or a barotropic initial condition for the flow. As the background term would strongly call back the $\sf_2^0$ toward one of these options, the overall dynamics would be notably impacted by this decision. Indeed, according to~\cite{hurlburt1986}, instantiating a two-layer model by setting the initial sublayer field at rest yields not better results regarding surface forecast than a regular RG model, thus emphasizing the need for a precise a priori estimation of sublayer initial state. Our regularization however, does not constrain the value of the sublayer stream function itself but rather its dynamics: it has to somehow satisfy a pseudo-advection of PV in the bottom layer, but as for its value it remains unconstrained. Additionally, this method allows for more flexibility regarding subsurface flow: if improving surface dynamics requires drifting away from the advection of the sublayer (truncated) PV (through the introduction of non-QG patterns in the reconstructed field for example), it can do so. A strictly enforced two-layer QG model would not be able to do so without the introduction of an external forcing term in the sublayer advection equation.

\subsection{Deformation radius correction}\label{sec:deformation-radius-correction}

Other than the truncation error, incorrect model stratification parameters (in the $H_i$ or the $g_i$) could result in mismatch in the surface flow dynamics. Key characteristics of the flow dynamics are the so-called barotropic and first baroclinic deformation radii, which, in the multilayer QG framework, can be computed from the eigenvalues of the stretching matrix $\mat{A}$. In order to increase the robustness of our two-layer-like model, we wish to allow for baroclinic deformation radius adjustments. Given the diagonalization of the two-layer stretching matrix,

\begin{equation}
    \mat{A}= \mat{C}_{\mathrm{M2L}}\begin{bmatrix}\lambda_{\rightleftarrows} & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_{\rightrightarrows}\end{bmatrix}\mat{C}_{\mathrm{L2M}},
\end{equation}

where $\lambda_{\rightleftarrows}$ is the baroclinic-related eigenvalue and $\lambda_{\rightrightarrows}$ the barotropic one, and $\mat{C}_{\mathrm{M2L}}$ (resp., $\mat{C}_{\mathrm{L2M}}$) the modes to layers (resp., layers to modes) passage matrix. The baroclinic radius correction is performed by introducing the parameter $\alpha$, and the modified stretching matrix,

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        \tilde{\mat{A}}_{\alpha} & = \mat{C}_{\mathrm{M2L}}\begin{bmatrix}\left(1+\alpha\right)\lambda_{\rightleftarrows} & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_{\rightrightarrows}\end{bmatrix}\mat{C}_{\mathrm{L2M}}, &\\
        & = \mat{A} + \alpha\lambda_{\rightleftarrows}\mat{C}_{\mathrm{M2L}}\begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix}\mat{C}_{\mathrm{L2M}},
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}

which coincides with the default stretching matrix when $\alpha=0$. The barotropic deformation radius then remains unchanged as it is linked to the total depth which can be quite easily approximated whereas the baroclinic deformation radius, $L_{\rightleftarrows}$ becomes

\begin{equation}
    L_{\rightleftarrows} = \frac{1}{f_0 \sqrt{\left(1+\alpha\right)\lambda_{\rightleftarrows}}}.
\end{equation}

Our model implementation replaces $\mat{A}$ by $\tilde{\mat{A}}_{\alpha}$ in the usual two-layer QG equations and the parameter $\alpha$ is added in the control vector, granting one additional degree of freedom for the variational assimilation.

\section{Method validation}

We have considered two validation experiments. In the first one, we have set a double-gyre experiment using a three-layer QG model (QG3L). Therefore, the reconstruction error from the RG model (dynamical interpolation situation) is mostly explained by the truncation of the QG equations. Within this framework, we can also assess the ability of SurfML model in reconstructing top layer dynamics and compare its performances with VarDyn. Two variants of this setup have been explored. One where model parameters (reference layer depth, reduced gravity values\dots) are the same in the reference QG model and in the RG counterpart. This configuration also allows a fair comparison for $\tilde{\sf}_2$ and $\sf_2$. In the other variant, model parameters are modified to assess the robustness of each of the models when dealing with incorrect baroclinic deformation radius. The second validation experiment uses surface fields from the eNATL60 simulation~\parencite{brodeau2020} as the reference flow. This experiment investigate the capability of our model in reproducing a flow that present ageostrophic, complex and rich dynamical features.

In both experiments, observations are drawn from the surface using an observing system simulation experiment that simulates SWOT-like sampling tracks.

As this work focuses on introducing a new parameterization to reduce model error, we have decided to only consider such parameterization in the control vector. Indeed, since all tested frameworks (RG, VarDyn, SurfML) share identical initial and boundary conditions, differences in performances only results from model error and its correction. Ideal initial and boundary conditions are therefore retrieved from the surface flow of the reference simulation. Furthermore, the estimation of initial and boundary conditions through variational assimilation has already been widely investigated~\parencite{cong1998,zou1995,gunson1996,seiler1993,ayoub2006}, suggesting that our methodology could quite easily be adapted to a more realistic data assimilation configuration.

Assimilation is conducted on 20 days-long periods. Over these periods, satellite passages are simulated and, when a track spans over the considered domain, observation loss is evaluated using the collected data. Regarding the SurfML model, regularization loss is evaluated at every time step. The VarDyn regularization however, is implemented so that wavelet scales are penalized following a -5/3 power law. Small scales are therefore penalized more strongly than large scales.

VarDyn parameters have been chosen so that the one layer RG model matches the baroclinic deformation radius of a two-layer QG model. The value of this deformation radius is about \qty{29}{\kilo\meter}, similar to the one described in~\cite{leguillou2023}, namely \qty{30}{\kilo\meter}. The parameter of the RG model however are set so that the one-layer dynamics matches the one of the truncated QG model.

During the variational assimilation process, all parameterized field are instantiated at 0. Therefore, SurfML's initial observation loss is the exact same as the RG one since the bottom layer appears to be at rest. Loss minimization is performed using Pytorch's implementation of the Adam optimizer~\parencite{kingma2017}. Optimization was stopped after 200 iterations as longer optimization did not yield any significant improvement in the loss function for each of the models.

After the assimilation process is completed, model performances will be evaluated using the normalized Root Mean Squared Error (nRMSE). Given a field $\chi$ and its reference value $\chi_{\mathrm{ref}}$, the corresponding nRMSE is

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{nRMSE}\left(\chi, \chi_{\mathrm{ref}}\right) = \sqrt{\frac{{\lVert \chi - \chi_{\mathrm{ref}} \rVert}^2}{{\lVert \chi_{\mathrm{ref}} \rVert}^2}}.
\end{equation}

The error normalization is such that evaluating the nRMSE of 0 over a reference field yields 1. A nRMSE greater than 1 indicates that predicting 0 would actually improve the score.


\subsection{Multilayer QG}

Since the reference simulation is forced by wind to display a double-gyre, we also include the wind as an external forcing within the advection equations for all models, denoting $\tau = \transpose{\begin{bmatrix} \tau_x & \tau_y \end{bmatrix}}$ the wind stress tensor, the advection equation rewrites

\begin{equation}
    \pdvt{q^{RG}} + \gradperp \sf_1 \cdot \grad {\pv}^{RG} = F + \gradperp \cdot \tau
\end{equation}

where $F$ represent the additional forcing that depends on the frameworks,

\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        F_{\mathrm{RG}} & = 0, \\
        F_{\mathrm{VarDyn}} & = \epsilon, \\
        F_{\mathrm{SurfML}} & = \frac{-f_0^2}{H_1 g_2}\Dt{\tilde{\sf}_2}.
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}

As the reference was established over a $\qty{2560}{\kilo\meter}$ by $\qty{5120}{\kilo\meter}$ domain, we have decided to focus on subdomains of the said area. We selected two $\qty{640}{\kilo\meter}$ x $\qty{1280}{\kilo\meter}$ zones, Z1, Z2

\subsection{eNATL60}

Using surface fields from the eNATL60 simulation~\parencite{brodeau2020}, we monitor SurfML's reconstruction of a fluid with ageostrophic dynamics. We have focused on a single area of dimension $\qty{680}{\kilo\meter}$ x $\qty{1030}{\kilo\meter}$ centered on $\qty{55}{\degree}$W $\qty{40}{\degree}$N.
Parameters for all models are detailed are the exact same as the ones from~\cref{tab:qg3l-model-parameters}, apart from Coriolis parameters which were set to $f_0 = \qty{9.373}{\per\second}$ and $\beta = \qty{1.753}{\per\meter\per\second}$. Surface stream function is inferred from eNATL60's SSH values. First,  SSH anomaly $ SSH - {\langle SSH \rangle}_{T,XY}$ is computed by removing the time-space average (evaluated over the 20 days period) from the SSH field. We then use the Drakkar Forcing Set 5.2~\parencite{dussin2016} to retrieve the atmospheric pressure anomaly $p_{\mathrm{atm}} - {\langle p_{\mathrm{atm}} \rangle}_{XY}$, (${\langle \cdot \rangle}_{XY}$ denoting the spatial-average operator). Finally, surface stream function $\Psi$ verifies

\begin{equation}
    \Psi_{\mathrm{surf}} = \frac{1}{f_0 \rho_0} \left( p_{atm} - {\langle p_{\mathrm{atm}} \rangle}_{XY} \right) + \frac{g}{f_0}\left( SSH - {\langle SSH \rangle}_{T,XY} \right),
\end{equation}

where $\rho_0$ is the reference density for water.

As the resulting stream function contain small-scale features, it is then smoothed using a Gaussian filter and then downsampled from the high-resolution, curvilinear coordinates of the eNATL60 simulation into the regular, Cartesian grid of the QG models. It is this smoothed, down-scaled field that is used for both initial and boundary conditions of the QG models. The observations however are sampled from the non-filtered surface stream function field, using the same OSSE as for the three-layer QG comparison. A total of 10333 pixels are observed during a single assimilation cycle. Control vectors size are as follows

Results show that SurfML still outperforms VarDyn in reconstructing the top layer flow in a realistic configuration. \section{Discussion}

The improvement of surface flow reconstruction achieved by the SurfML method highlights the benefits of introducing a parameterization for the sublayer stream function. Indeed, such parameterization allows for a significant reduction of the model error induced by the truncation of the multilayer QG equations. The introduction of a physics-based regularization also appears to significantly improve the model performances, as it softly enforces multilayer-like dynamics while still allowing for some flexibility on the sublayer flow.

Although SurfML reaches almost perfect surface reconstruction in the idealized three-layer QG framework, its reconstruction of the sublayer stream function is not as accurate. If we were to focus on the sublayer flow reconstruction, we could look for a reduced-order space that encourages physically-relevant patterns for the subsurface stream function. However, enforcing such a QG structure might prevent the model from generalizing to more realistic flows, for which the identification of the parameterized field to the sublayer stream function might not be relevant. Indeed, as the eNATL60 experiment shows, a significant part of the realistic flow is still not captured by the SurfML method. Rather than enforcing a QG structure through appropriate reduced-order space, improvement might be achieved by digging into the next-order terms of the QG approximation~\parencite{muraki1999}. Despite identifying the parameterized field to the sublayer stream function is a natural choice in the multilayer QG framework, such identification can be challenged in a realistic flow. As the QG PV can be obtained through a truncation of the asymptotic decomposition of Ertel's PV~\parencite{muller1995}, the parameterized field might rather be identified to the missing terms of said truncation, or rather to the terms transported by the geostrophic surface flow. Given that the realistic surface contains ageostrophic components, the methodology could be improved by emulating such terms. This could be done through the explicit introduction of additional parameterized fields, or through a combination of the SurfML and VarDyn methodologies, considering that the SurfML parameterization would tackle the truncation error while leaving to VarDyn's forcing all the remaining unresolved processes. 

Rather than introducing additional field to the model's control vector, another option would be to integrate external forcing into the method. Bottom topography for example, can have a significant impact on the flow dynamics and can not be taken into account by the RG framework (as pointed out by~\cite{hurlburt1986}). By considering the flow to be two-layered, it is possible to modify the SurfML formalism to include a topography-related term in the regularization.

As all the presented results were established using simulated data, the next step would be to apply the SurfML method to real observation. Although the eNATL60 simulation is realistic enough to grant confidence into the model's ability to handle real data, moving to such data yields notable challenges. Especially, initial and boundary conditions on the surface stream function would have to be addressed, whether estimated through climatology or gridded products, or included into the control vector.

This new method highlights the benefits of deriving model error parameterization from the general physical framework of the model. The use of physics-based regularization has also proven relevant, especially in the absence of any prior estimate of the parameterized field, which can be very useful when there are no direct observations of the true state of the field. Successfully applying SurfML to real SWOT data would be a significant step forward, assessing the relevancy of the method in a real-world application.
